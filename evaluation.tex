\subsection{Experimental Setup}
% 我们的实验一共在两个Cluster上进行。在Cluster A上，我们在72F CPU上评估了MFold的性能。同时，我们将Cluster B上运行的FastFold{\color{red}~\cite{}}作为我们的基线。每个Cluster的具体配置如表~\ref{tab:cluster}所示。
We evaluate our optimizations on two distinct computing environments. The primary environment is a CPU cluster that serves as the optimization target of this work. The cluster is equipped with two 72F processors, each consisting of 8 NUMA nodes. Unless otherwise stated, all reported CPU results are obtained on a single 72F processor, which allows us to highlight the effectiveness of our optimizations without the influence of inter-socket communication overhead. This platform provides not only high computational throughput but also specialized matrix computation units, which we exploit to accelerate AlphaFold2 inference.

For comparison, we benchmark against FastFold, a state-of-the-art GPU-accelerated implementation of AlphaFold2. FastFold experiments are conducted on a server equipped with two Intel(R) Xeon(R) Gold 6336Y processors and a single NVIDIA A100 GPU. This configuration represents the mainstream deployment setting for AlphaFold2 inference on GPUs and provides a strong baseline to evaluate the competitiveness of our CPU-based optimizations.

Our evaluation dataset consists primarily of protein sequences from CASP14, which is widely adopted as a standard benchmark for protein structure prediction. Using CASP14 ensures that our results are comparable with prior studies and faithfully reflect AlphaFold2's predictive capability under realistic inference workloads.

It is worth noting that our optimized implementation achieves inference performance on a single 72F processor that matches or even surpasses that of an A100 GPU running FastFold, highlighting the potential of CPU-based acceleration with matrix units and quantization techniques.

We first evaluate the end-to-end inference performance of AlphaFold2 across different implementations and numerical precisions. The benchmark is conducted on the CASP14 dataset, which serves as a representative workload for large-scale protein structure prediction. We compare four variants: the baseline FP32 implementation on CPUs, the FP16 and INT8 quantized versions optimized for matrix units, and FastFold running on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU.

Figure [X] reports the average inference time per sequence for each method. The results clearly show that quantization provides significant performance benefits on the CPU platform. In particular, the INT8 implementation achieves the highest speedup, outperforming the FP32 baseline by a substantial margin and even approaching, or in some cases surpassing, the performance of FastFold on the A100 GPU. The FP16 implementation also demonstrates notable acceleration over FP32, although the gains are less pronounced than INT8.

Notably, on X\% of CASP14 inputs, the INT8 implementation running on a single 72F processor achieves shorter inference time than FastFold on an A100 GPU, with an average speedup of Y× over the FP32 CPU baseline.

These findings highlight the effectiveness of our quantization and matrix-unit optimizations in narrowing the performance gap between CPU and GPU platforms. By combining low-precision computation with NUMA-aware execution, our optimized CPU implementation enables end-to-end inference throughput that makes CPU-based deployment of AlphaFold2 highly competitive.

\subsection{Attention Kernel Performance}
To better understand the performance gains at the operator level, we conduct a detailed evaluation of the attention kernel, one of the most computationally demanding components of AlphaFold2’s Evoformer module. We benchmark the kernel across a series of representative input shapes that reflect typical multiple sequence alignment (MSA) lengths and residue counts in CASP14.

Figure [Y] presents the measured execution times for FP32, FP16, and INT8 attention kernels on the 72F CPU platform. The results indicate that INT8 consistently achieves the lowest runtime across all tested input sizes, benefiting from both reduced arithmetic cost and lower memory bandwidth requirements. FP16 also provides clear improvements compared to FP32, though its advantage diminishes for smaller problem sizes where memory overhead dominates.

These results confirm that quantization directly accelerates the most critical kernel in AlphaFold2, which in turn translates into substantial end-to-end performance improvements. Moreover, the consistent scalability of INT8 attention kernels across diverse input shapes demonstrates the robustness of our optimization approach to real-world protein sequence variability.
\subsection{Accuracy Analysis}
% 我们使用了CASP14中的所有数据对比了int8、fp16以及fp32精度下的AlphaFold2的预测精度。我们选用的评估标准为TM-Score
To assess the impact of quantization on prediction accuracy, we adopt TM-score~\cite{zhang2004scoring} as the evaluation metric. TM-score is a widely used measure to quantify the structural similarity between two protein conformations, with values ranging from 0 to 1. A higher TM-score indicates greater similarity, and scores above 0.5 are generally considered to represent the same overall fold, while scores above 0.7 are typically regarded as highly reliable in structural biology studies. The TM-score of two structures can be calculated using the TM-align tool~\cite{zhang2005tm}.

In our evaluation, we use the FP32 version of AlphaFold2 predictions as the reference baseline. The quantized models in FP16 and INT8 are evaluated by aligning their predicted protein structures against the FP32 results using the TM-align tool, after which TM-scores are computed. This allows us to directly quantify the structural consistency of low-precision predictions relative to the high-precision baseline.

{\color{red}Figure [X]} presents the TM-scores of predictions on the CASP14 dataset, comparing FP16 and INT8 models against the FP32 baseline. The results show that both FP16 and INT8 predictions maintain TM-scores well above 0.7 for the vast majority of sequences, indicating that the reduced precision does not compromise the reliability of structural predictions.

To further illustrate the robustness of our quantization approach, {\color{red}Figure [Y]} depicts a visual comparison of predicted protein structures for a representative sequence (denoted as {\color{red}sequence X}). The overlay of FP32, FP16, and INT8 predictions demonstrates high structural consistency, with only negligible deviations observed in flexible loop regions.

These results confirm that our quantization strategy preserves the accuracy of AlphaFold2 while significantly improving inference efficiency. In particular, the INT8 quantized model achieves reliable structural predictions comparable to FP16 and FP32, thereby validating the feasibility of low-precision execution on CPU matrix units.

\subsection{Scalability Evaluation}
To further assess the scalability of our optimized CPU implementation, we evaluate the end-to-end inference performance under varying degrees of NUMA parallelism. Specifically, we measure the runtime of AlphaFold2 using 1, 2, 4, and 8 NUMA nodes on a single 72-core processor, while varying both the input sequence length and the numerical precision (FP32, FP16, INT8).

The experiments are conducted on a representative subset of sequences drawn from CASP14, categorized by length into short, medium, and long sequences (exact length ranges to be specified). For each category, we report the average inference time across the different NUMA configurations and precision modes.

Figure [Z] presents the scalability curves. Across all sequence lengths, we observe that the inference time decreases nearly linearly with the number of NUMA nodes, indicating good parallel efficiency of our implementation. In particular, the INT8 version consistently achieves the fastest runtime for all NUMA settings, benefiting from both reduced computation and better cache utilization. FP16 also exhibits substantial improvement over FP32, especially for longer sequences where arithmetic intensity is higher.

Importantly, the scalability trends remain stable across different sequence lengths, demonstrating that our NUMA-aware design generalizes well to diverse input sizes. For long sequences, the combination of INT8 quantization and full 8-NUMA parallelism yields the highest throughput, making it feasible to process even challenging CASP14 targets entirely on CPUs with competitive latency.

These results validate that our approach not only improves single-threaded and single-NUMA performance, but also scales efficiently across the entire socket, enabling flexible resource allocation depending on the workload and system constraints.
