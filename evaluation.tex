We evaluate our optimizations on two distinct computing environments. The primary environment is a CPU cluster that serves as the optimization target of this work. The cluster is equipped with two 72F processors, each consisting of 8 NUMA nodes. Unless otherwise stated, all reported CPU results are obtained on a single 72F processor, which allows us to highlight the effectiveness of our optimizations without the influence of inter-socket communication overhead. This platform provides not only high computational throughput but also specialized matrix computation units, which we exploit to accelerate AlphaFold2 inference. Detailed hardware and software configurations are shown in Table~\ref{tab:cluster}.

For comparison, we benchmark against FastFold, a state-of-the-art GPU-accelerated implementation of AlphaFold2. FastFold experiments are conducted on a server equipped with two Intel(R) Xeon(R) Gold 6336Y processors and a single NVIDIA A800 GPU. This configuration represents the mainstream deployment setting for AlphaFold2 inference on GPUs and provides a strong baseline to evaluate the competitiveness of our CPU-based optimizations.

Our evaluation dataset consists primarily of protein sequences from CASP14, which is widely adopted as a standard benchmark for protein structure prediction. Using CASP14 ensures that our results are comparable with prior studies and faithfully reflect AlphaFold2's predictive capability under realistic inference workloads.

It is worth noting that our optimized implementation achieves inference performance on a single 72F processor that matches or even surpasses that of an A800 GPU running FastFold, highlighting the potential of CPU-based acceleration with matrix units and quantization techniques.

\subsection{Impact of Matrix Dimensions on Performance}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/gemm_sensitivity.pdf}
    \caption{Performance sensitivity of GEMM kernels on the 72F platform. (a) Impact of $K$ ($M=N=32$): Throughput increases significantly with $K$ as the compute cycles amortize the register readout overhead. INT8 demonstrates superior scalability due to higher peak throughput. (b) Impact of $N$ ($M=K=32$): Performance remains stagnant, indicating that increasing the output matrix size cannot overcome the latency bottleneck induced by a small reduction dimension ($K$).} 
    \label{fig:gemm_sensitivity}
\end{figure}
To quantify the architectural bottlenecks identified in Section~\ref{sec:analysis}, we conduct a microbenchmarking study by independently sweeping the reduction dimension ($K$) and the independent dimension ($N$). Figure~\ref{fig:gemm_sensitivity} illustrates the measured throughput for both FP16 and INT8 precisions.

\textit{Reduction Dimension ($K$) Dominance:}
As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:gemm_sensitivity}(a), we fix $M=N=32$ and vary $K$ from 32 to 2048. The results demonstrate a strong positive correlation between $K$ and performance. At small $K$ ($K=32$), the system is severely bound by the register readout overhead, yielding only $\sim$120 GFLOPS. However, as $K$ increases, the arithmetic intensity rises, effectively amortizing the fixed readout costs. Consequently, FP16 throughput saturates at 718 GFLOPS ($6.1\times$ speedup), while INT8 throughput scales almost linearly to 1685 GFLOPS ($13.7\times$ speedup), leveraging the higher peak compute capability of integer units.

\textit{Insensitivity to Independent Dimension ($N$):}
Conversely, Figure~\ref{fig:gemm_sensitivity}(b) isolates the impact of $N$ by fixing the bottleneck dimension $K$ at 32. Strikingly, performance remains stagnant around 120 GFLOPS regardless of $N$ (ranging from 32 to 2048). This "flatline" behavior confirms that increasing parallelism along the independent dimension fails to mitigate the bottleneck caused by frequent register array draining. These empirical results corroborate our analysis: performance on the 72F architecture is strictly determined by the amortization efficiency of the $K$ dimension.

\subsection{End to End Performance Comparison}
\label{sec:end_to_end}
% 我们选择了一系列不同长度的输入数据上评估了目前的sota FastFold以及FP16、INT8精度的MatrixFold，以评估MatrixFold在不同长度的序列上的加速效果。我们将序列长度按照1K以下、2K左右以及2K以上划分为短序列、长序列以及极长序列，分别进行了评估。在评估过程中，对于FastFold我们使用了一块80GB的A800，而对MatrixFold则使用了1颗72F CPU。
We evaluate the end-to-end inference performance of our proposed MatrixFold against the state-of-the-art GPU implementation FastFold. Experiments are conducted across a diverse set of protein sequences with varying lengths. Following prior practice, we categorize the inputs into three groups: \textit{short sequences} ($<$1K residues), \textit{long sequences} (around 2K residues), and \textit{extremely long sequences} ($>$2K residues). For FastFold, we use an NVIDIA A800 GPU with 80GB memory, while MatrixFold is executed on a single 72F CPU. We further evaluate MatrixFold under both FP16 and INT8 precision modes to assess the impact of quantization on inference speed.

Table~\ref{tab:inference_ex_long} summarizes the raw performance results for extremely long sequences.
On extremely long sequences, MatrixFold significantly outperforms FastFold. This advantage stems from the fundamental hardware differences: the 72F CPU provides substantially larger main memory capacity compared to the 80GB HBM of the A800 GPU. FastFold is forced to use very small chunk sizes in order to fit the sequences into GPU memory, which restricts parallelism and reduces throughput. In contrast, MatrixFold benefits from abundant system memory, allowing the same degree of parallelism as in shorter sequences, thereby sustaining high efficiency.

For extremely long sequences, INT8 precision delivers notable speedups over FP16 within MatrixFold. This is because longer sequences correspond to larger matrix dimensions, which fully exploit the high throughput of INT8 OPA instructions. Moreover, in AlphaFold's computation, many GEMMs involve reductions along the sequence dimension. As the reduction dimension $K$ grows with sequence length, the relative impact of array-register data movement diminishes, and overall computational efficiency increases.

The evaluation results on short sequences are presented in Figure~\ref{fig:endtoend}. A key observation is that when the sequence length is relatively short, the performance differences between FastFold, MatrixFold with FP16 precision, and MatrixFold with INT8 precision are not substantial. On extremely short sequences, FastFold demonstrates a slight advantage. This can be attributed to the parallelism constraints within a single Non-Uniform Memory Access (NUMA) node. Specifically, the cores within one NUMA node may not be fully utilized for these shorter sequences, limiting the parallel processing capability of MatrixFold. In contrast, FastFold is able to leverage the full parallelism of the GPU. Forcing MatrixFold to scale across multiple NUMA nodes in this scenario would introduce communication overhead that negates the benefits of increased parallelism.

As illustrated in Table~\ref{tab:inference_ex_long}, the performance landscape changes dramatically for extremely long sequences. Here, MatrixFold significantly outperforms FastFold. With a sequence length of 4096, MatrixFold achieves a 3.2$\times$ speedup over FastFold when using FP16 precision. This considerable advantage stems from the memory capacity of the CPU platform, which is far greater than the memory available on an A800 GPU. Consequently, FastFold is constrained to use a very small chunk size, which in turn curtails its parallelism and degrades inference performance. MatrixFold, however, can process these long sequences with the same degree of parallelism as shorter ones, thanks to the ample available memory.

An interesting finding is the noticeable performance degradation of INT8 precision compared to FP16 for these long sequences. This is because the larger matrix sizes associated with longer sequences necessitate that the quantization operator traverses the matrix before the GEMM operation. This traversal disrupts spatial locality, meaning that the processor is less likely to access memory locations that are physically close to each other, leading to less efficient memory access patterns and a subsequent reduction in speed.

To assess the impact of quantization on prediction accuracy, we adopt TM-score~\cite{zhang2004scoring} as the evaluation metric. TM-score is a widely used measure to quantify the structural similarity between two protein conformations, with values ranging from 0 to 1. A higher TM-score indicates greater similarity, and scores above 0.5 are generally considered to represent the same overall fold, while scores above 0.7 are typically regarded as highly reliable in structural biology studies.~\cite{xu2010significant} The TM-score of two structures can be calculated using the TM-align tool~\cite{zhang2005tm}.
In our evaluation, we use the FP32 version of AlphaFold2 predictions as the reference baseline. The quantized models in FP16 and INT8 are evaluated by aligning their predicted protein structures against the FP32 results using the TM-align tool, after which TM-scores are computed. This allows us to directly quantify the structural consistency of low-precision predictions relative to the high-precision baseline.
Figure~\ref{fig:tmscore} presents the TM-scores of predictions on a representative dataset from CASP14~\cite{casp14}, comparing FP16 and INT8 models against the FP32 baseline. The evaluation dataset contains real protein sequences of varying lengths, ensuring the validity of the results. The results show that both FP16 and INT8 predictions maintain TM-scores well above 0.7 for the vast majority of sequences, indicating that the reduced precision does not compromise the reliability of structural predictions. Nevertheless, a noticeable degradation in accuracy can still be observed under INT8.

To further illustrate the robustness of our approach, Figure~\ref{fig:visual_compare} depicts a visual comparison of predicted protein structures for a representative sequence (denoted as T1024). It can be observed that under FP16, the predicted structures are highly consistent with the ground truth, with secondary structures and other fine details being well preserved. In contrast, under INT8, although the atomic-level arrangement remains relatively close to the ground truth, the structural details are poorly maintained, and distinct secondary structures are largely unrecognizable.
% 可以看出，在FP16下，预测的结构与真实结构高度一致，其二级结构等细节也得到了很好的保留。在INT8下，虽然原子结构与真实结构相比较为接近，但预测的结构细节保持较差，基本无法识别明显的二级结构。
The overlay of FP32, FP16, and INT8 predictions demonstrates high structural consistency, with only negligible deviations observed in flexible loop regions.
These results confirm that our mixed-precision strategy preserves the accuracy of AlphaFold2 while improving inference efficiency under FP16 precision. %In particular, the INT8 quantized model achieves reliable structural predictions comparable to FP16 and FP32, thereby validating the feasibility of low-precision execution on CPU matrix units.


\subsection{Scalability Evaluation}
\begin{figure*}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figures/a.pdf}
    \caption{\color{red}Scalability of MatrixFold on a single 72F processor under varying NUMA parallelism and sequence lengths.} 
    \label{fig:scala}
\end{figure*}
To further assess the scalability of our optimized CPU implementation, we evaluate the end-to-end inference performance under varying degrees of NUMA parallelism. Specifically, we measure the runtime of AlphaFold2 using 1, 2, 4, and 8 NUMA nodes on a single 72F processor, while varying both the input sequence length and the numerical precision (FP16, INT8). We use the sequence length from 512 to 1576 to cover a broad range of practical workloads.

Figure~\ref{fig:scala} presents the scalability curves. Across all sequence lengths, we observe that the inference time decreases nearly linearly with the number of NUMA nodes, indicating good parallel efficiency of our implementation. 
% 从图中可以得出，随着序列长度的增加，我们可以观测到FP16与INT8的性能由接近开始变为FP16优于INT8。这里的原因与~\ref{sec:end_to_end}中提到的一致，当序列长度增加时，矩阵规模变大，而在进行GEMM之前的量化算子需要遍历整个矩阵，这破坏了空间局部性。
From the figure, it can be observed that as the sequence length increases, the performance of FP16 and INT8, which is initially comparable, diverges, with FP16 outperforming INT8. The underlying reason is consistent with the discussion in Section~\ref{sec:end_to_end}: as the sequence length grows, the matrix size becomes larger, and the quantization operator must traverse the entire matrix before GEMM, thereby disrupting spatial locality.

% Importantly, the scalability trends remain stable across different sequence lengths, demonstrating that our NUMA-aware design generalizes well to diverse input sizes. For long sequences, the combination of INT8 quantization and full 8-NUMA parallelism yields the highest throughput, making it feasible to process even challenging CASP14 targets entirely on CPUs with competitive latency.

These results validate that our approach not only improves single-threaded and single-NUMA performance, but also scales efficiently across the entire socket, enabling flexible resource allocation depending on the workload and system constraints.
