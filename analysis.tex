\section{Bottlenecks Analysis}
Despite its algorithmic advances, AlphaFold2 exhibits several computational bottlenecks when executed on modern hardware. To systematically identify optimization opportunities, we analyze performance, memory, and communication characteristics using representative sequence lengths.
\subsection{Performance Breakdown}
We first profile AlphaFold2 on a sequence of length $N_s = 1024$, which is a representative workload for practical inference. The computational time is dominated by the \textbf{Evoformer} module, which accounts for approximately \textbf{75\%} of the total execution time. The \textbf{Structure module} contributes around \textbf{13\%}, while the \textbf{Embedding stage} is relatively lightweight at \textbf{11\%}.

This breakdown highlights two important characteristics. First, the Evoformer block, composed primarily of attention and triangular update operations, is the key optimization target, as improvements here directly translate into overall performance gains. Second, although the Structure and Embedding modules are less dominant, they remain non-negligible for end-to-end efficiency, especially on CPU platforms where pipeline imbalance may exacerbate idle times.
\subsection{Memory Analysis}

The memory footprint of AlphaFold2 is primarily determined by attention activations. For single-sequence attention, the input activations scale as $O(N_s \times N_r)$, while for pairwise attention they scale as $O(N_r^2)$, where $N_s$ denotes sequence length and $N_r$ denotes residue count. The hidden dimension in these modules is relatively modest—typically 128 or 256—which means that model weights occupy only a small fraction of memory. In contrast, activations grow quadratically with sequence length, and thus dominate memory usage.

A further challenge arises from the computation of attention logits. For single-sequence attention, the logits scale as $O(N_s \times N_r^2)$, whereas for pairwise attention they scale as $O(N_r^3)$. Such growth can quickly exceed practical memory limits, especially on GPUs with fixed HBM capacity. To mitigate this, AlphaFold2 employs chunking~\cite{liu2022swin,kitaev2020reformer} strategies to partition computations into smaller tiles. While chunking reduces peak memory consumption, it also decreases parallelism and may negatively impact throughput, particularly when implemented on hardware platforms with high parallel capacity such as GPUs. On CPUs with large memory capacity, however, larger chunk sizes can often be maintained, avoiding these trade-offs.

\subsection{Communication Analysis}
Another major bottleneck in AlphaFold2 arises from communication overheads. To enforce the physical plausibility of the protein structure, AlphaFold2 interleaves different types of attention and pairwise updates, many of which require switching tensor layouts. For example, intra-chain and inter-chain attention operate on different axes of the same tensor, leading to repeated transpose operations. These operations require frequent tensor transpositions to convert between row-major and column-major layouts of activations. Since AlphaFold2 parallelizes primarily along the sequence or residue dimensions ($N_s$ or $N_r$), every transposition following an attention step introduces a synchronization barrier across parallel workers. In Figure~\ref{fig:af2model}, the transposition is denoted by \textit{T}, and \textit{T'} represents an equivalent transposition operation.

Consequently, the Evoformer incurs a large number of communication events, particularly when executed on many-core architectures. Each of these events involves data movement across NUMA domains or across nodes in a distributed setup, introducing latency and bandwidth pressure. As sequence lengths increase, the cost of these transpositions grows super-linearly with activation size, making communication one of the most significant bottlenecks for scaling AlphaFold2.
